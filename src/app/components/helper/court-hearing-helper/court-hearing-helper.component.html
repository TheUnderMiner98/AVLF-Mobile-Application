I need to prepare for court hearing...

Anxious about representing yourself at court? Let's get ready now!


Show up on time

When is my court date?

Call the Fulton County Magistrate Court Dispossessory Division (404-613-5360) or check the court database twice a week to check for your court date and courtroom.

What if I can't make it to court on that day?

If you don't show up, you will receive a Default Judgment. This means that the court will immediately approve a Writ of Possession for your landlord to evict you, and you will be required to pay the full amount your landlord claims you owe.



Prepare for court

How to prepare evidence

Keep any and all evidence you have. Your dispossessory warrant, a copy of your lease, photographs, printed screenshots of texts or emails, paperwork, or subpoenaed witnesses. Be sure your evidence is ready and organized so you can present it to a judge or in mediation quickly when asked. You may upload them into system before the court date.

Remember to set aside time for your court

Set aside the whole morning or afternoon for your court day. There is no way to say for sure how long it can take. There are many cases happening at the same time, and there can be unexpected delays.



Court day

What is going to happen in court?

When you appear in court, you will first go to mediation to resolve your case without a judge.

What does it mean to sign an agreement?

If you reach an agreement with your landlord in mediation, the mediator will write it down, and you, the landlord, and the judge will sign the agreement.


You have to keep your end of the agreement or you may risk eviction.


Remember! Once you sign the agreement, it is binding and cannot be appealed or changed after the fact. Be sure you can live with the terms before signing it.

When will I see a judge?

If you do not reach an agreement in mediation , you will return to the courtroom and go before a judge on the same day.

Represent myself in court

It can be intimidating to represent yourself in court, but remember that you have the right to be there contesting your eviction!


Here are some tips on how to represent yourself in court:


1. Have all your evidence organized and ready


2. Listen carefully to what the judge is saying


3. When the landlord or the judge is speaking, do not interrupt. You will have your turn to speak. Take notes so you can remember!
